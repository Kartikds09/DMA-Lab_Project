What is Liver Cirhosis?

Cirrhosis of the liver is permanent scarring that damages your liver and interferes with its functioning. It can lead to liver failure. Cirrhosis is the result of persistent liver damage over many years. Alcohol and drugs, viruses and metabolic factors are the most common causes.

It is the late stage liver disease, in which healthy liver tissue has been gradually replaced with scar tissue. This is a result of long-term, chronic hepatitis. Hepatitis is inflammation in your liver, which has many causes. When inflammation is ongoing, your liver attempts to repair itself by scarring. But too much scar tissue prevents your liver from working properly.

What are the different stages of liver cirrhosis?

    It mainly has 3 major stages:

  1. Compensated Cirrhosis:

    2. In this stage, the liver is still able to perform its essential functions despite the presence of significant scarring. Patients may not experience symptoms initially, or they may have mild symptoms such as fatigue or weakness. However, liver function tests may indicate abnormalities. Complications such as varices (enlarged veins in the esophagus or stomach) may develop in this stage.


  2. Decompensated Cirrhosis:

    3. This stage occurs when the liver is significantly damaged and can no longer perform its functions adequately. Decompensated cirrhosis is characterized by the development of complications such as ascites, hepatic encephalopathy, variceal bleeding, and jaundice. Symptoms may become more pronounced and can include abdominal swelling, confusion, easy bruising or bleeding, and yellowing of the skin and eyes (jaundice).


  3. End-Stage Cirrhosis:

    4. Also known as terminal cirrhosis or advanced cirrhosis, this stage is characterized by severe liver dysfunction and failure. The liver's ability to function is severely impaired, leading to life-threatening complications such as hepatorenal syndrome (kidney failure secondary to liver failure), severe ascites, profound hepatic encephalopathy, and increased susceptibility to infections. Liver transplantation may be the only treatment option for individuals with end-stage cirrhosis.
